## The Thesis (One Paragraph)
Standard Black will build a fund that **acquires and rolls up HVAC and plumbing companies** — and only those two trades. Buy established, cash-flowing local service businesses at trades-business multiples (2.5–4x EBITDA), combine them under one back-office and brand, install professional management and AI-driven systems, and create a regional platform worth a higher multiple than the sum of its parts. This is the operating-company sleeve of the portfolio (Vertical 5), and it becomes the next major venture after the real estate note fund is generating reliable cash flow.

## Why HVAC & Plumbing Only (the focus discipline)
We deliberately cut construction, trucking, and property management from the active target list. Reasons:

- **Non-discretionary, recession-resistant demand.** When the AC dies in a Texas summer or a pipe bursts, it gets fixed regardless of the economy. The cash flow holds through downturns — exactly what the hedge-fund downside lens demands.
- **Recurring revenue, not one-time projects.** Service contracts, maintenance agreements, and repeat customers create durable, predictable cash — unlike construction's lumpy project work.
- **Fragmented, owner-aged market.** Thousands of small "one-owner-and-a-truck" shops run by operators nearing retirement with no succession plan. That's the ideal roll-up supply — motivated sellers, no institutional competition at the small end.
- **Real, local moats.** The moat is density + reputation + recurring contracts, not technology you can't build. Win a metro, own it.
- **Adjacent and combinable.** HVAC and plumbing share customers, dispatch logistics, technicians' skill overlap, and back-office. One platform can cross-sell and consolidate both trades — they reinforce each other instead of splitting focus.
- **Margin upside under better management.** Most small shops have no real systems — no CRM, no dispatch optimization, no pricing discipline, no marketing engine. Standard Black systems + AI are the value-creation lever.

**Cut and why:** Construction (cyclical, lumpy, project-risk), Trucking (capital-heavy, thin margins, driver dependency), Property management (different skill set, lower margins, distraction). They can return later as the platform matures — but not now. Focus is the whole point.

## The Roll-Up Model (how value is created)
1. **Platform acquisition** — buy one solid "anchor" company first (strong cash flow, retainable management or installable operator) as the base to build on.
2. **Bolt-ons** — acquire smaller shops in the same metro and fold them into the platform's back-office, brand, and systems.
3. **Multiple arbitrage** — buy small companies at ~3x EBITDA; a consolidated multi-location platform with clean books and management depth sells at a meaningfully higher multiple. The spread is the profit.
4. **Operational lift** — standardized pricing, dispatch software, recurring-service membership programs, AI for scheduling/lead-handling/follow-up, shared marketing. Raise EBITDA on what you already own.
5. **Geographic density** — own a metro before expanding, so brand, reviews, and route efficiency compound.
