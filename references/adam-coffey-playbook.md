# Adam Coffey's Systems — and How Standard Black Applies Them

**Created:** 2026-05-31
**Why he matters to us:** Coffey is the closest real-world blueprint for the HVAC/plumbing roll-up. He was a 3-time CEO of national PE-backed service companies for 9 sponsors, completed **58 acquisitions**, averaged **~5x MOIC** at exit, and ran **CoolSys** (HVAC/refrigeration) — growing revenue **239%** and EBITDA **376%** in 4 years, combining 21 companies, then flipping it from ~$200M → $400M+ → $1B+. He literally did, at scale, what we plan to do small.

## System 1 — The 30-20-10 Rule (his core operating standard)
Every dollar of revenue must produce:
- **≥ 30% gross profit**
- **< 20% SG&A** (overhead)
- **≥ 10% net profit**

His non-negotiable rule: **if it doesn't work at one truck, it never works at 50 or 500.** Most businesses fail because they chase revenue instead of unit economics and assume scale will fix margins. It won't. Profitability has to be true at the smallest unit first.

**How we apply it:**
- It becomes a **screening filter** in the comps tracker — any target that can't hit (or be fixed to hit) 30-20-10 is suspect. Add it to the disqualifier logic.
- It's the **post-acquisition operating standard** — when we buy a shop, the first job is getting every truck to 30-20-10, then add more trucks.
- It reframes the whole game: we're buying **earnings quality**, not revenue. Ties directly to the hedge-fund "cash is truth" lens.

## System 2 — Multiple Arbitrage (the engine of the return)
Buy small companies cheap, combine them, sell the platform at a premium multiple:
- A ~$1M-EBITDA shop trades at ~**5x**.
- Put 8 together into a clean $10M+ EBITDA platform and it trades at **10–14x**.
- **The spread between buy-multiple and sell-multiple is the profit** — on top of the EBITDA growth itself. This is *why* the roll-up works mathematically.

**How we apply it:** This is the core of our thesis. Buy DFW/Phoenix shops at 2.5–4x, build a clean multi-location platform, and the 27+ PE consolidators (Apex, Sila, Wrench, etc.) become our exit at a much higher multiple. Coffey explains the exact math we're betting on.

## System 3 — The Staged Growth Framework (from *Empire Builder*)
Different stages require a different CEO:
- **$0–$10M:** Replicate what works and add services. Nail the model. (← *where we start — one anchor shop*)
- **$10M–$100M:** Grow through M&A — acquire competitors, partner with PE capital. (← *the roll-up phase*)
- **$100M–$1B+:** Stop micromanaging; steer through **systems and processes**. Lead the leaders.

**How we apply it:** Tells us what skill Kerry/Kody need at each phase. Phase one is operational discipline (30-20-10). The roll-up phase is a *deal-and-integration* skill. The later phase is a *systems* skill. Don't try to run a $50M platform like a one-truck shop.

## System 4 — Valuation = a Function of Perceived Risk
Two companies with identical EBITDA can sell at wildly different multiples. The premium goes to the one with:
- **Durable, recurring earnings** (maintenance contracts)
- **Diversified customers** (no concentration)
- **Mature systems** (runs without the owner)

**His key insight: reducing risk raises value more reliably than chasing revenue.** A buyer pays up for *certainty*.

**How we apply it:** This is exactly our comps-tracker scorecard — recurring contracts, customer concentration, owner-dependency. When we own shops, we increase exit value by *de-risking* them (systematize, diversify, build recurring revenue) — not just growing the top line. It also tells us what *we* should pay up for when buying.

## System 5 — Margin Expansion Without Raising Prices
Grow EBITDA by getting more efficient with the revenue you already have — process optimization, dispatch efficiency, reducing SG&A — not by hiking prices or chasing new customers.

**How we apply it:** Most small shops have zero systems. Our value-creation lever post-acquisition is operational: dispatch software, route density, pricing discipline, AI for scheduling/lead-handling. Raise EBITDA on what we already bought → directly feeds the multiple arbitrage.

## System 6 — Exit Is an Engineered Multi-Year Process, Not an Event
Owners who treat the sale as a last-minute transaction leak value and lose leverage. Winners professionalize operations early, build clean books, and create **multiple paths to liquidity** to preserve negotiating power.

**How we apply it:** Build the platform *to be sold* from day one — clean financials, real management depth, audited-quality books, recurring revenue. Start with the exit in mind so we're never a forced/desperate seller. (This is the whole premise of his *Exit-Strategy Playbook*.)
