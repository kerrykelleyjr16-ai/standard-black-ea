## 1. What Standard Black Capital Corp Is

Standard Black Capital Corp (SBCC) is the institutional corporate arm of the Standard Black enterprise. It is not the family holding company. It is not the note fund. It is the capital and acquisition platform — the vehicle that builds business credit at scale, retains and redeploys earnings, owns operating companies, and positions Standard Black for institutional capital and strategic optionality.

**Its six jobs:**

| Job | Description |
|---|---|
| Hold retained earnings | Profits from subsidiaries and operations compound inside the corporate structure at the 21% federal corporate rate — not passed through |
| Reinvest into acquisitions | SBCC is the parent that buys, owns, and manages operating companies |
| Build business credit | The entity itself builds a credit profile separate from Kerry's personal credit — lines, facilities, and eventually institutional debt |
| Own operating-company platforms | HVAC, property management, construction, trucking, and future acquisitions sit as subsidiaries under SBCC |
| Issue preferred equity | SBCC can raise institutional capital by issuing preferred stock to investors while the family retains common equity and control |
| Prepare for strategic exit | C-Corp structure is the prerequisite for a strategic sale, merger, reverse merger, or public-company-style structure |

## 2. Why a C-Corporation (Not an LLC)

SBCC must be a **C-Corporation**, not an LLC. This is the most important structural decision for this entity.

| Need | LLC | C-Corp |
|---|---|---|
| Multiple classes of equity (preferred + common) | Limited | Native capability |
| Outside investor equity (institutional) | Complicated | Standard and expected |
| Business credit facilities at scale | Possible but limited | Full access |
| SBA loans and corporate credit | Available | Full access |
| Retained earnings at corporate tax rate | Pass-through only | 21% federal rate |
| Strategic sale or M&A readiness | Complicated conversion needed | Ready as-is |
| Reverse merger / public structure | Requires conversion | Ready as-is |
| Investor familiarity and legal precedent | Limited | Universally understood |

The one tradeoff: C-Corps have double taxation on dividends (corporate tax + personal tax when distributed). The solution is to not distribute dividends early — **retain and reinvest**. That's exactly what SBCC is designed to do.

## 3. Jurisdiction: Delaware

Incorporate SBCC in **Delaware**, not Texas.

- Delaware corporate law is the most developed and investor-familiar in the country.
- The Court of Chancery is a specialized business court — disputes are resolved by judges, not juries.
- All institutional investors, PE firms, and M&A advisors expect Delaware.
- No sales tax in Delaware.
- Register as a foreign corporation in Texas (where you operate) — ~$750 one-time filing.

**Full legal name:** Standard Black Capital Corp
**State of incorporation:** Delaware
**Operating state:** Texas (foreign qualification)

## 5. Capital Stack

### Class A Common Stock — Family Ownership
- Owned by Kasino Family Holdings LLC.
- Carries all voting rights.
- This class never dilutes in a way that loses control.
- Kerry's family retains the company regardless of how much preferred equity is raised.

### Authorized Preferred Stock (Unissued Until Needed)
- Authorized at incorporation but not issued until a raise is structured.
- Standard terms: liquidation preference, non-cumulative or cumulative dividend, conversion rights, anti-dilution protection.
- Used to bring in investors at a defined valuation without giving up common equity or voting control.
- Series A, Series B structure as the company scales.

### No debt securities at formation.
Corporate credit comes through banking relationships and the SBA — not securities issuance.

## 6. Business Credit Roadmap

This is the path from zero to institutional credit. It takes 12–24 months done correctly. Do not skip tiers.

### Foundation (Month 0 — Before Any Credit)
- Delaware C-Corp incorporated, EIN issued
- Business address established (not Kerry's home address — use a registered agent or virtual office)
- Dedicated business phone number and email
- Business bank account opened (Chase, Bank of America, or a community bank with SBA lending relationships)
- 30–60 days of banking history before applying for anything

### Tier 0 — Business Identity (Month 1–2)
- Register with Dun & Bradstreet: get a **D-U-N-S Number** (free, takes 30 days)
- Register with Equifax Business and Experian Business
- Verify SBCC listing is accurate on all three bureaus
- Google Business profile, website, and professional email on company domain
- **Why this matters:** lenders check these. An entity with no presence is invisible and unfundable.

### Tier 1 — Vendor / Net-30 Accounts (Month 2–4)
Open net-30 accounts with vendors that report to business credit bureaus. Pay every invoice early — not just on time.

Recommended starter accounts (all report to D&B):
- **Uline** (shipping and office supplies)
- **Quill** (office supplies)
- **Grainger** (industrial and tools — relevant for operating companies)
- **Crown Office Supplies**
- **HD Supply** (relevant for property management and construction subsidiaries)

Pay net-30 invoices within 10 days every time. After 3–4 months, D&B Paydex score climbs to 80+.

### Tier 2 — Business Credit Cards (Month 4–8)
Once D&B Paydex is 80+ and bank account shows 3–6 months of history:

- **Capital One Spark** — good for businesses with limited history
- **Brex** — no personal guarantee, based on company financials
- **American Express Business** — strong rewards; targets established revenue

Keep utilization below 30%. Pay in full monthly. These cards report to business credit bureaus, not personal — critical for separating business and personal credit profiles.

### Tier 3 — Bank Credit Lines (Month 8–18)
Once Tier 2 is established and business bank account shows consistent activity:

- **Business line of credit** — $25k–$100k range initially; revolving; use for operating capital
- **SBA 7(a) loan** — up to $5M; designed for acquisitions and working capital; best rates of any business loan program
- **SBA 504 loan** — for real estate or equipment acquisitions; lower down payment requirements
- **Community Development Financial Institutions (CDFIs)** — alternative to banks for early-stage businesses; often minority-focused and more flexible

Kerry's note: SBA loans require 2 years of business tax returns. The clock starts at incorporation. Form SBCC now even if you don't need SBA financing yet — the 2-year clock runs regardless.

### Tier 4 — Corporate Credit Facilities (Month 18–36)
Once the operating subsidiaries are producing revenue and SBCC has a track record:

- **Revolving credit facilities** — negotiated directly with banks based on SBCC's financials
- **Acquisition financing** — used specifically to fund operating company purchases (HVAC, etc.)
- **Mezzanine debt** — subordinated debt with equity-like terms; used for larger acquisitions
- **Private credit** — debt capital from private credit funds; available once SBCC has EBITDA history

## 7. Operating Company Acquisition Platform

SBCC is the parent platform for all operating company acquisitions. Each acquired business becomes a wholly-owned subsidiary.

```
Standard Black Capital Corp
    |
    |---- Standard Black HVAC Holdings LLC (subsidiary)
    |         |---- [Acquired HVAC company] (DBA or sub-subsidiary)
    |
    |---- Standard Black Property Management LLC (subsidiary)
    |         |---- [Acquired PM company]
    |
    |---- Standard Black Construction Holdings LLC (subsidiary)
    |
    |---- Standard Black Trucking Holdings LLC (subsidiary)
```

**Why subsidiaries instead of direct SBCC ownership:**
- Liability isolation — one subsidiary's problems don't threaten SBCC or other subsidiaries
- Clean accounting and reporting per business unit
- Easier to sell individual subsidiaries without unwinding the whole structure
- Each subsidiary can have its own local bank relationships, insurance, and credit

**Acquisition targets (priority order per the portfolio playbook):**
1. Property management companies — recurring revenue, scalable, low capital intensity
2. HVAC companies — strong cash flow, recession-resistant, high demand
3. Construction companies — higher risk but strong margins
4. Trucking — highly scalable but capital-intensive and operationally complex

**Acquisition criteria (starting framework):**
- Established business with 3+ years of operating history
- Owner-operated (retiring owner = motivated seller)
- EBITDA positive
- Defensible customer base (contracts or recurring service relationships)
- Pricing: 3–5x EBITDA for service businesses; adjust for growth and margin quality
- Financing: SBA 7(a) as primary instrument; seller note for gap financing

## 8. Retained Earnings Strategy

SBCC does not distribute profits early. It retains and redeploys.

**The compounding machine:**
```
Operating subsidiary generates EBITDA
    → Pays management fee to Standard Black Family Office Mgmt LLC
    → Net earnings flow up to SBCC
    → SBCC pays 21% federal corporate tax on retained earnings
    → After-tax earnings held in SBCC treasury
    → Treasury deployed into: next acquisition | paying down debt | business credit building
```

**When to distribute:**
- Only when the family structure needs the capital for something specific.
- When SBCC has excess capital above its acquisition reserve target.
- When the tax math favors a qualified dividend or return of capital strategy.
- Not before. Early distribution defeats the purpose of the structure.

## 9. Preferred Equity Issuance (When Ready)

SBCC is authorized to raise outside capital through preferred stock. This is not a Day 1 activity — it happens once SBCC has a track record: 1–2 operating subsidiaries, documented revenue, and a clear use of proceeds.

**How it works:**
- SBCC issues Series A Preferred Stock to investors at a defined valuation.
- Preferred shareholders get: liquidation preference, dividend rights (cumulative or non-cumulative), conversion rights into common stock.
- Common stock (held by Kasino Family Holdings → trust) retains all voting control.
- Kerry's family never loses the company.

**Use of proceeds from a preferred raise:**
- Acquisition capital for the next operating company
- Expansion of existing subsidiaries
- SBCC operating reserve

**Compliance note:** issuing preferred equity to outside investors is a securities offering. This requires securities counsel, proper exemptions (Reg D 506(b) or 506(c)), and investor documentation before a dollar is raised.

## 10. Long-Range Strategic Optionality

The C-Corp structure and institutional credit profile SBCC builds over time creates three exit and growth options that LLCs cannot access:

**Option 1 — Strategic Sale**
A PE firm or strategic buyer acquires SBCC or a portfolio of its subsidiaries. Delaware C-Corp, clean cap table, audited financials, and a portfolio of cash-flowing operating companies = institutional-grade acquisition target.

**Option 2 — Reverse Merger / SPAC**
SBCC merges into a public shell company or SPAC, gaining public company status without a traditional IPO. This gives access to public equity markets and institutional investors. Requires SEC reporting compliance once public.

**Option 3 — Direct Listing or IPO**
Long-range, high-bar option. Requires significant revenue scale, institutional investors already on cap table, and a strong advisory team. Delaware C-Corp is the prerequisite.

**The timeline:** none of this happens before Phase III (6–10 years). But the decisions made at formation — Delaware C-Corp, authorized preferred stock, clean cap table — are what make these options available later. You can't retrofit this structure easily. Build it right the first time.
