# Company Evaluation Frameworks — The Hedge Fund Lens & The VC Lens

**Purpose:** Teach Kerry how to evaluate a company the way a seasoned 20-year hedge fund manager and a seasoned 20-year VC manager would — and how to fuse the two into a single discipline for acquiring operating companies under Standard Black / Kasino Family Holdings.

**Use with:** `.claude/skills/acquisition-analyzer/SKILL.md` (the operational scorecard) and `references/long-term-portfolio-playbook.md` (the strategy this feeds).

---

## The One Idea to Anchor Everything

A business is worth the **cash it will hand you over its life, discounted for time and risk.** Everything below is just two different disciplines for answering one question:

> *"What is this thing actually going to put in my pocket, how sure am I, and what am I paying for that certainty?"*

The hedge fund manager and the VC are both answering that question. They just weight the variables differently because they hunt in different waters.

---

## Part 1 — The Hedge Fund Manager (The Downside Lens)

A hedge fund manager who has survived 20 years has lived through crashes, frauds, and value traps. Their entire psychology is built around one instinct: **protect capital first, make money second.** They assume the seller knows more than they do and that the story is too optimistic.

### How they think
- **Margin of safety.** Buy a dollar of value for fifty cents. The gap between price and value *is* the protection. No gap, no trade.
- **Downside first.** "If everything goes wrong, how much do I lose?" They size the bet to survive being wrong.
- **Cash is truth, earnings are opinion.** Accounting profit can be engineered. Cash in the bank cannot. They trust the cash flow statement over the income statement.
- **Mean reversion & skepticism.** Sky-high margins attract competition and fall. A "one-time" problem is often recurring. The burden of proof is on the optimist.
- **Be the house, not the gambler.** Take bets where the math is in your favor and you get paid to hold risk others won't.

### What they actually examine

**1. Quality of Earnings (QoE) — is the profit real?**
- Does reported net income turn into actual cash? (Compare net income to operating cash flow over 3 years. Profit with no cash = red flag.)
- Are earnings recurring or one-time? Strip out asset sales, insurance payouts, owner add-backs that won't repeat.
- **Owner add-backs** (huge in small-business deals): sellers "add back" personal expenses run through the business (cars, travel, family on payroll) to inflate EBITDA. Verify every one. This is where small deals get mispriced.
- Working capital: is the business consuming cash to grow (bad for a buyer) or generating it?

**2. Valuation — what am I paying vs. what I get?**
- **EBITDA multiple:** Price ÷ EBITDA. Trades businesses typically trade 2.5–4x. Pay 6x for a 3x business and you've lost before you start.
- **Free Cash Flow yield:** annual free cash ÷ purchase price. A $1M business throwing off $250K/yr = 25% yield. Compare that to any other use of the money.
- **DCF (discounted cash flow):** project future cash, discount it back to today's value. The honest version of "what's it worth." Don't over-engineer it — garbage projections in, garbage value out.
- **Comps:** what did similar businesses actually sell for? Asking price means nothing; *transaction* price is the signal.
- **Asset/liquidation floor:** what is it worth broken up and sold for parts? That's your true downside.

**3. Durability of cash flow — will it still be here in 10 years?**
- Revenue quality: recurring contracts and repeat customers > one-time project work.
- Customer concentration: one client = >30% of revenue is a loaded gun. If they leave, the business dies.
- Balance sheet: how much debt? Debt turns a bad year into a bankruptcy.
- Cyclicality: does it collapse in a recession? (Construction does; HVAC repair holds up better — people fix the AC regardless.)

**4. The downside scenario (always run it)**
- Lose your biggest customer — do you still cover debt?
- Revenue drops 30% — are you still solvent?
- The owner walks and takes relationships with them — what's left?

### The hedge fund question set (memorize these)
1. What's the free cash flow, and is it real?
2. What multiple am I paying, and how does that compare to comps and to FCF yield?
3. What has to go *wrong* for me to lose money, and how likely is it?
4. Where's my margin of safety — what's the asset floor if the business stops?
5. What's the debt, and can the cash flow service it through a bad year?

## Part 2 — The Venture Capital Manager (The Upside Lens)

A 20-year VC has learned the opposite lesson from the hedge fund manager. Most of their bets go to zero — and it doesn't matter, because **one winner returns the whole fund.** This is the **power law.** Their psychology is built around asymmetry: limited downside (you can only lose 1x your money), unlimited upside (you can make 100x).

### How they think
- **Slug it, don't shave it.** They don't optimize the entry price by 10%. They ask: "Can this be 50x?" If yes, paying a bit more doesn't matter. If no, the cheap price doesn't save you.
- **Bet on the jockey, not just the horse.** The team and the operator matter more than the current numbers, because the plan *will* change. They invest in people who figure it out.
- **Market size is destiny.** A great company in a tiny market is a small outcome. A decent company in a massive, growing market can become enormous. TAM (total addressable market) first.
- **Moats compound.** They hunt for businesses that get *stronger* with scale — network effects, brand, switching costs, proprietary advantage.
- **Embrace asymmetry.** Be wrong often if being right pays 50:1.

### What they actually examine

**1. Market — how big can this get?**
- **TAM / SAM / SOM:** Total Addressable Market (everyone who could buy), Serviceable (who you can realistically reach), Obtainable (who you'll actually win). Is the market big and *growing*?
- Tailwinds: demographics, regulation, technology shifts working *for* the business.
- "Why now?" What changed that makes this the moment?

**2. The team / operator — can they win?**
- Founder/operator quality: grit, integrity, domain mastery, ability to recruit and lead.
- For an *acquisition*: does the business survive the owner leaving? (A VC bets on the owner staying; an acquirer needs the business to live *without* them — this is the key inversion for Kerry.)
- Can you install a better operator (Kody, or a hired GM) and unlock value?

**3. The moat — why won't this get crushed?**
- Switching costs, brand, network effects, regulatory licenses, location/density advantage, proprietary process or data.
- For trades businesses the moat is usually **local density + reputation + recurring service contracts** — not technology.

**4. Scalability — does it get better or worse as it grows?**
- Unit economics: does each new customer/job make money after fully loaded cost?
- Operating leverage: do margins *expand* with scale, or does cost grow just as fast?
- The roll-up thesis: can you buy 5 of these, combine back-office, and create something worth more than the sum? (This is the core PE/family-office play for trades businesses — and your eventual model.)

**5. The upside scenario (always run it)**
- With Standard Black systems, AI, and better management, what does this look like in 3 years?
- Can it be a platform you bolt other acquisitions onto?
- What's the 10x version of this story, and what would have to be true for it to happen?

### The VC question set (memorize these)
1. How big is the market, and is it growing or shrinking?
2. Why does this business win — what's the moat, and does it strengthen with scale?
3. Who runs it, and does it survive (or improve) if I change the operator?
4. What's the realistic upside if I run it better — and is it a platform or a one-off?
5. What has to be true for this to be a 5–10x outcome?

## Part 3 — Fusing the Two (The Standard Black Discipline)

Acquiring cash-flowing operating companies (HVAC, plumbing, trucking, property management) is **not** pure hedge fund and **not** pure VC. It's the search-fund / private-equity middle — and the elite operators there use **both lenses in sequence:**

> **Underwrite the downside like a hedge fund. Underwrite the upside like a VC. Buy only when both say yes.**

### The sequence
1. **Hedge fund gate first (don't lose money).** Is the cash flow real? Is the price cheap relative to FCF? Does it survive a bad year and the owner leaving? *If it fails here, stop — the upside doesn't matter if you can go to zero.*
2. **VC lens second (can this grow).** Big market? Defensible? Can a better operator and Standard Black systems multiply it? Is it a platform?
3. **Structure the deal to get both.** The best acquirers don't just pick good companies — they *engineer* asymmetry into the terms:
   - **Seller financing** — seller carries part of the price, paid from the business's own cash flow. Lowers your capital at risk and keeps the seller honest.
   - **Earnout** — part of the price is paid only if the business hits targets. You don't overpay for a story that doesn't materialize.
   - **Owner transition period** — keep the seller 6–12 months to transfer relationships and prevent the business dying when they leave.

### The unified evaluation order (run every deal through this)
| Step | Lens | The question |
|---|---|---|
| 1. Quality of earnings | Hedge fund | Is the profit real cash, or accounting/add-back fiction? |
| 2. Valuation | Hedge fund | Cheap on FCF yield and EBITDA multiple vs. comps? |
| 3. Downside | Hedge fund | Do I survive losing the top customer / a 30% drop / the owner? |
| 4. Market | VC | Big and growing, with tailwinds behind it? |
| 5. Moat | VC | Why doesn't this get competed away — and does it strengthen with scale? |
| 6. Operator & upside | VC | Survives a new operator? Can I 3–5x it? Is it a platform? |
| 7. Structure | Both | Can I use seller financing / earnout to cut risk and bake in upside? |

If steps 1–3 fail, walk away regardless of the upside. If 1–3 pass but 4–6 are weak, it's a cash-flow hold, not a growth play — pay accordingly. If all seven line up, that's a deal worth pursuing.

## Part 4 — Universal Red Flags (Both Managers Run From These)
- **Net income that never becomes cash** — the single biggest tell of a problem.
- **Seller won't share clean financials** (or only has them "in their head"). No books, no deal.
- **Customer concentration** — one client >30% of revenue.
- **Total owner dependency** — the owner *is* the business; relationships and know-how walk out the door with them.
- **Aggressive add-backs** padding EBITDA to justify the price.
- **Declining industry or shrinking local market.**
- **Deferred maintenance / hidden capex** — equipment about to need replacement, debt you'll inherit.
- **A price set by the seller's emotion or retirement number**, not by the math.
- **Pressure to move fast / "other buyers waiting."** Urgency is a sales tactic, not your friend.

## Part 5 — How to Build the Skill (Practice Plan)
1. **Read the cash flow statement first, every time** — before the pitch, before the story. Train the hedge fund reflex.
2. **Write the downside case before the upside case.** Force yourself to argue against the deal first.
3. **Build a comps file.** Every trades-business sale you see — price, revenue, EBITDA, multiple. Over time you'll *know* what cheap looks like without calculating.
4. **Practice on deals you won't buy.** Run BizBuySale listings through the unified order above. Reps build instinct.
5. **Always run both scenarios in writing:** "If it goes wrong, I lose ___. If it goes right, I make ___." If you can't write both, you don't understand the deal yet.
6. **Log every evaluation in `decisions/log.md`** — even the passes. Pattern recognition is the whole game, and it compounds.

## The Two Mantras
- **Hedge fund:** *"Rule #1: don't lose money. Rule #2: don't forget Rule #1. Price is what you pay; value is what you get."*
- **VC:** *"Limited downside, unlimited upside. Bet on the operator and the market. One winner pays for all the misses."*

Hold both at once. Acquire when the downside is protected *and* the upside is real.
