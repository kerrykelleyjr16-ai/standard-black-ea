# Long-Term Portfolio Playbook

**Prepared for Mr. Kelley**  
**Date:** April 21, 2026

## Purpose

Create one clean portfolio blueprint that ties together capital reserves, note-fund formation, public-market exposure, crypto, operating-company acquisitions, AI-enabled operations, and long-range family-office architecture.

## 1. Executive Overview

This playbook converts the main themes of your long-term portfolio project into one reference document: build cash reserves first, use residential mortgage notes as the central cash-flow engine, expand into a repeatable fund platform, add operating-company acquisitions over time, and ultimately consolidate the whole structure into a family-office style system.

The design principle throughout is simple: prioritize ownership, cash flow, downside control, and future sellability. The goal is not just to collect assets, but to build an institutional-quality platform that can scale across funds, operating businesses, and family governance.

### Core long-range objectives

- Establish a durable personal and operating reserve base before taking unnecessary portfolio risk.
- Build and scale a mortgage note platform that can grow from a first fund into a multi-fund ecosystem.
- Acquire service-based operating businesses such as HVAC, plumbing, construction, and related companies.
- Use AI and system design to keep the human team lean while increasing throughput and consistency.
- Transition over time from individual deal execution into family-office style ownership, governance, and capital allocation.

## 2. Portfolio Architecture

Your recurring portfolio hierarchy places liquidity and staying power first, then scalable cash-flow assets, then diversification and selective upside, then direct operating-control bets.

| Priority | Bucket | Primary role | Practical interpretation |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Cash reserves | Liquidity, defense, optionality | Personal runway, operating reserves, legal/admin reserves, and capital ready for opportunity |
| 2 | Real estate notes / fund platform | Core cash-flow engine | Performing and reperforming first-lien notes form the anchor of the long-term compounding model |
| 3 | Public markets | Liquid diversification | Long-duration stock exposure complements the private-asset book without becoming the core identity |
| 4 | Crypto | Selective asymmetric upside | Use conviction sizing and risk caps rather than letting crypto dictate the plan |
| 5 | Operating companies | Control, cash flow, strategic synergies | Acquire businesses that can compound cash flow, create jobs, and support the larger ownership structure |

### Operating principles

- Favor structures that preserve control, flexibility, and clean reporting.
- Do not let optional upside crowd out reserve discipline.
- Use separate sleeves, entities, or dashboards when risk profiles are meaningfully different.
- Keep the architecture understandable enough that future investors, lenders, and family decision-makers can follow it.

## 3. Multiphase Build Path

| Phase | Time frame | Main objective | What must be true before advancing |
|---|---|---|---|
| Phase I | 0–2 years | Build reserves, sharpen note underwriting, form the base entity stack, and begin small-scale execution | Cash discipline exists; power team is forming; first underwriting and asset-management systems are repeatable |
| Phase II | 3–5 years | Launch Fund I or equivalent pooled structure; prove distributions, reporting, and underwriting discipline | Track record exists; LP communications are consistent; reserve logic and servicing oversight work in practice |
| Phase III | 6–10 years | Expand into additional funds, SPVs, or sleeves while adding first operating-company acquisitions | Management bandwidth expands without losing control; acquisition criteria are disciplined; reporting remains clear |
| Phase IV | 11–15 years | Integrate funds, holdings, and operators into a more formal holding-company or family-office architecture | Governance, treasury, succession, and tax/legal coordination become proactive rather than reactive |
| Phase V | 16–25 years | Consolidate into a legacy structure focused on scale, continuity, multigenerational control, and durable capital allocation | The platform can function beyond founder-only decision-making and withstand leadership transitions |

## 4. Fund Platform Blueprint

The residential mortgage note platform is the clearest current center of gravity inside the project. The fund strategy is built around a cash-flow-first identity with a controlled workout sleeve rather than a broad distressed-debt identity.

### Fund I launch design

- **Core thesis:** Acquire discounted 1st-lien residential mortgage notes with a cash-flow-first core and a limited workout sleeve for controlled upside.
- **Starting mix:** 85% Income Sleeve / 15% Workout Sleeve.
- **Income Sleeve job:** Generate steady, financeable cash flow and support routine LP distributions.
- **Workout Sleeve job:** Create controlled upside through selected cures, modifications, settlements, deed-in-lieu outcomes, foreclosure progression, or REO exits.
- **LP design:** Quarterly distributions, 8% preferred return, and an 80/20 LP-GP profit split after return of capital.
- **Why it works:** The strategy emphasizes pricing discipline, underwriting discipline, servicing discipline, and bounded downside rather than complexity.

### Income Sleeve buy-box essentials

- 1st lien only; single-family and 2–4 unit residential assets.
- Non-condo and owner-occupied preferred, with tenant-occupied assets allowed when payment support is clear.
- Target UPB roughly $40K–$350K, with lower balances only when fixed costs still make sense.
- Minimum property value about $70K; target as-is LTV generally 75% or less.
- Performing standard: current and at least 9 of last 12 payments; reperforming standard: ideally 6 consecutive on-time payments.
- Escrowed taxes and insurance preferred, with non-escrowed assets requiring verified current status.

### Workout Sleeve buy-box essentials

- 1st lien only; SFR and 2–4 unit assets only.
- Target UPB roughly $30K–$300K, with low-value files avoided when fixed costs absorb too much recovery.
- Prefer bids at 60% or less of as-is value; stretch only when cure probability is unusually strong.
- Each asset must fit a defined path: cure/mod, settlement, deed-in-lieu, foreclosure already in progress, or a clean REO exit.
- Avoid broken-title files, heavy litigation, and broad bankruptcy complexity in the first fund.

### Required underwriting discipline

1. Tape screen  
2. Collateral file and enforceability review  
3. Conservative valuation  
4. Title, tax, lien, municipal, HOA, and probate review  
5. Borrower and servicing reality check  
6. Legal timeline and cost model  
7. Financial model with yield, IRR, downside cases, and max bid  
8. One-page IC memo  

## 5. Business Acquisition Platform

Your operating-company lane is not random diversification. It is a control-and-cash-flow sleeve that can eventually reinforce the larger capital stack.

### Recurring target sectors

- HVAC
- Plumbing
- Construction
- Property management
- Trucking
- Related service businesses

### Acquisition scorecard

| Category | Good target signals | Red flags to avoid early |
|---|---|---|
| Demand profile | Essential service, recurring need, fragmented market | Fad demand or one-time project dependency |
| Owner dependence | Team can run core work with light founder oversight | Everything depends on seller relationships or personal labor |
| Cash flow quality | Clean books, stable margins, defendable add-backs | Messy books, unexplained adjustments, customer concentration |
| Operational upside | Dispatch, pricing, service agreements, follow-up, and CRM can improve | No clear path to better systems or margins |
| Integration risk | Simple handoff, manageable headcount, plain-English operations | Legal disputes, chaotic crews, weak controls, excessive complexity |

## 6. AI-Enabled Operating Model

- Use AI for first-pass underwriting support, IC memo drafts, research packaging, CRM follow-up, and reporting templates.
- Keep a small human core focused on judgment, approvals, relationships, capital, and exception handling.
- Standardize decision frameworks so junior staff or AI agents can operate from the same buy boxes and checklists.
- Design every recurring workflow so it can be audited, repeated, and improved over time.

## 7. Family-Office End State

The long-term destination is not simply a larger portfolio. It is a coordinated ownership architecture that can hold funds, operating companies, reserves, trusts, and future family governance in one system.

- Nested ownership and entity discipline around funds, SPVs, operating companies, and reserve vehicles.
- A treasury function that distinguishes operating cash, reserves, distributions, and reinvestment capital.
- Documented governance around succession, family roles, lending policies, and capital allocation guardrails.
- An investment platform that remains legible to investors, lenders, counsel, and future family stakeholders.

## 8. Annual Review Checklist

- Are cash reserves still first, or has opportunity chasing started to erode liquidity discipline?
- Does the note platform still match its stated identity: cash-flow-first with controlled workout upside?
- Are underwriting, servicing, and reporting systems getting stronger as complexity increases?
- Do new acquisitions improve the platform, or do they create distraction without durable control value?
- Can every major asset or vehicle be explained clearly to an outside investor, lender, or family successor?
- Is the organization becoming more institutional, or is it still too dependent on founder memory and improvisation?
